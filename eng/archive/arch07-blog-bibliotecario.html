Blog Bibliotecario

Archive of publications

This section gathers a long-form series of essays originally published in the blog Bibliotecario, spanning cultural history, librarianship, Indigenous epistemologies, visual archives, oral traditions, and the materiality of books and objects. Moving across geographies and historical periods, the texts examine how knowledge is preserved, distorted, transmitted, or silenced through manuscripts, photographs, musical instruments, textiles, architectural forms, ritual practices, and everyday artifacts. Rather than treating libraries as isolated institutions, the series situates them within wider ecosystems of memory and power, where archives intersect with colonial histories, technological change, extinction, resistance, and the fragile survival of voices at the margins.

(+) Abstract

This compilation brings together five texts that move between the irrecoverable sound of the past, the human presence behind artistic works, the history of typographic simulation, the memory carried by South American cemeteries, and the imagined voice of the wind. It opens with a reflection on ancient music and pre-recording sonic worlds, from pre-Columbian instruments to Baroque performance, vanished voices, historical soundscapes, and the future loss of our own audible present. It then turns to the traces left by artists in their works, using the author's childhood encounter with Dürer's Self-Portrait as a point of departure for thinking about gesture, intention, error, labor, and the desire to survive through memory. Another text reconstructs the function and history of Lorem ipsum, connecting pre-digital graphic design and printing practices with the long continuity of layout conventions from Renaissance typography to digital publishing. The compilation also evokes small cemeteries across northern Argentina, the Andes, and other South American landscapes, reading their plastic flowers, walls, colors, and exposed locations as signs of community presence, remembrance, and continuity in harsh or apparently empty territories. The final piece gives voice to the wind as a living, ancient, intelligent, and linguistically expressive being, present throughout human history but excluded from manuals of magical or legendary entities. Together, these texts explore how memory persists through sounds, images, design conventions, graves, names, materials, landscapes, and atmospheric presences, while returning to a shared concern: the need to look beyond the obvious object and recognize the lives, gestures, absences, and meanings attached to it.

This compilation brings together five texts that examine representation, survival, material culture, invented languages, and the political uses of memory across cinema, music, craft, genealogy, and ancient writing. It opens with a critique of how Indigenous peoples have been distorted by film, especially through Hollywood's repeated use of stereotypes, substitutions, linguistic inaccuracies, and colonial visual conventions, arguing that such images do not merely entertain but shape public understanding of Native societies and histories. It then turns to the kamacheña, a small flute from northern Argentina and southern Bolivia, presenting it as a fragile but persistent survival of older Andean musical layers, sustained by local builders, performers, dances, ritual calendars, and regional memory despite its near absence from commercial recordings and public visibility. A third text reflects on the construction of a square frame drum in Braojos, connecting hands-on instrument making with the recovery of traditional Spanish rural music, the transmission of craft knowledge, and the memories embedded in sound, materials, and collective practice. The compilation also follows the author's surname through Italian, Provençal, Catalan, and medieval documentary traces, using genealogy as a way to think about migration, linguistic transformation, family memory, and the long historical life of names. Another text reconstructs the history of Orba, a little-known Spanish proposal for a universal language published in 1893, situating it within the broader history of international auxiliary languages and their attempts to overcome linguistic fragmentation. The final essay focuses on the Assyrian lamassu of Kalhu, analyzing them as protective figures, imperial monuments, stolen archaeological objects, and supports for royal inscriptions that transformed power into durable memory. Together, these texts explore how images, instruments, names, languages, monuments, and inscriptions carry cultural meanings across time, while also showing how easily memory can be shaped, distorted, commodified, forgotten, or made to serve authority.

(+) Abstract

This compilation brings together six texts that examine endangered traditions, historical memory, cultural representation, material knowledge, and marginalized identities across several contexts. It opens with the recovery of the gaita serrana of Madrid's northern mountains, an almost vanished hornpipe once played by shepherds and now partially revived through surviving instruments, recordings, descriptions, and local musicians committed to restoring a rural soundscape broken by the loss of oral transmission. It then turns to Kitab al-Tabikh, the tenth-century Abbasid cookbook compiled by al-Warraq, presenting it not only as a collection of recipes, but as a documentary window into medieval Arab gastronomy, domestic knowledge, medical ideas, etiquette, manuscript survival, and the democratization of elite culinary practices. Another text reconstructs the history of the Carib community of Santa Rosa de Arima in Trinidad, focusing on the institution of the Carib Queen as a female authority responsible for sustaining Indigenous memory, ritual, and cultural continuity after colonization, missionization, displacement, and British rule. The compilation also explores the Chinese erhu, tracing its Central Asian origins, construction, playing technique, sonic qualities, and place in Chinese musical traditions, while emphasizing the persistence of artisanal knowledge within a modernized instrumental culture. A further essay reflects on Brian Dettmer's sculptural "autopsies" of old books, considering the transformation of obsolete encyclopedias into visual artworks as a second life for discarded printed matter rather than simple destruction. The final text critically examines the misattribution of several Bolivian urban-Andean musical genres as "Afro-Bolivian,"" distinguishing them from the actual saya of Afro-Bolivian communities in the Yungas and denouncing the commercial, academic, and popular distortions that appropriate and deform Black identity. Together, these pieces explore how fragile cultural forms survive, disappear, return, or are misrepresented, and how memory depends on the careful reading of instruments, books, recipes, bodies, names, performances, and the communities that keep them meaningful.

(+) Abstract

This compilation brings together five texts concerned with conflict, domestic knowledge, violence, language, and early documentary traces of Indigenous worlds in South America. It opens with the "War of Vicuñas and Basques" in seventeenth-century Potosí, reconstructing a violent colonial conflict shaped by mining wealth, ethnic and regional factions, class resentment, corruption, vendettas, and the struggle for control over the silver economy of the Cerro Rico. It then turns to an early twentieth-century New England recipe book, using its domestic instructions, preservation methods, cleaning advice, household remedies, and practical tricks as evidence of everyday knowledge transmitted through women's labor, oral memory, and domestic continuity. A third text examines the German Landsknechte, Renaissance mercenaries whose weapons, clothing, battlefield practices, camp life, brutality, mobility, and commercial logic reveal the transformation of European warfare from feudal obligation into professionalized violence for hire. The compilation then reflects on dictionaries, grammars, vocabularies, and linguistic sources as tools for cultural and ethnomusicological research, while warning against pseudo-etymological speculation, nationalist distortion, and the manipulation of Indigenous words to support predetermined historical narratives. The final text revisits Thomas Falkner's eighteenth-century description of Patagonia and adjacent regions, focusing on his account of Moluche, Puelche, Tehuelche, and related Indigenous cosmologies, ritual specialists, musical instruments, concepts of good and evil spirits, subterranean worlds, celestial geographies, and cultural practices that remain valuable despite the limits and biases of colonial observation. Together, these pieces explore how history survives through conflict records, recipes, weapons, words, missionary accounts, and oral traditions, while emphasizing the need to read every source critically: not as transparent truth, but as a fragment shaped by power, memory, language, interest, and the conditions of its preservation.

(+) Abstract

This compilation brings together six texts that examine technological transition, colonial representation, philosophical meaning, domestic knowledge, and the survival or distortion of memory across film, travel writing, everyday manuals, and cultural criticism. It opens with the early years of sound cinema, when Hollywood struggled to adapt spoken films for foreign audiences before dubbing and subtitling had been perfected, exploring international sound versions, foreign-language remakes, multilingual actors, accent problems, and the economic logic behind the first attempts to translate cinema for global markets. It then turns to an early twentieth-century Argentine travel chronicle on Potosí, reading it as a revealing document of its time: rich in descriptions of Andean markets, clothing, music, language, and popular life, but also saturated with exoticism, Hispanist ideology, colonial nostalgia, and distorted explanations of Indigenous culture. Another text presents translated fragments from Terry Eagleton's reflections on the meaning of life, focusing on modernity, capitalism, the privatization of values, the commodification of culture, the rise of substitute spiritualities, the social role of sport, and the contemporary difficulty of producing shared meaning. The compilation also revisits Catherine Parr Traill's nineteenth-century guide for women emigrating to Canada, treating it as a practical manual of survival and household management that records knowledge about clothing, food preservation, gardening, wild plants, domestic manufacture, cooking, fuel, livestock, seasonal work, and the material intelligence required for settler life. Together, these pieces examine how knowledge is translated, mediated, inherited, distorted, commercialized, or lost: in the technical solutions of early cinema, in the ideological filters of travel literature, in philosophical attempts to understand modern life, and in the practical skills once necessary for daily survival. Their common thread is the documentary value of apparently secondary materials, which reveal not only facts or techniques, but entire systems of perception, power, labor, memory, and cultural change.

(+) Abstract

This compilation brings together five texts that examine music, popular memory, craft, catastrophe, and documentary survival through instruments, calendars, industrial labor, maritime history, and shipwrecks. It opens with the history of the banjo, tracing the instrument from its African roots and its early use among enslaved communities in North America to its appropriation through minstrel shows, its later industrial and middle-class reinvention, and contemporary efforts to reconnect it with the Black and rural traditions from which it emerged. It then turns to Robert Chambers's The Book of Days and its account of Guy Fawkes' Day, using the calendar miscellany as a window onto nineteenth-century antiquarian culture, popular celebration, political memory, ritual violence, and the transformation of an anti-Catholic commemoration into a broader symbolic repertoire later reshaped by modern media. Another text revisits John B. Priestley's description of The Potteries, presenting the ceramic workshops of North Staffordshire as spaces where industrial production remained deeply rooted in manual skill, inherited technique, bodily knowledge, and craft pride. The compilation then reconstructs the wreck of the whaling ship Essex, attacked and sunk by a sperm whale in 1820, following the disaster through its links to Moby-Dick, the violence of the whaling industry, survival at sea, cannibalism, trauma, testimony, and the unstable border between historical fact and literary transformation. The final text examines the wreck of the Duchess of Albany on the coast of Tierra del Fuego, connecting missionary accounts, maritime routes, local geography, Selk'nam testimony, museum fragments, and the material remains of a ship still visible near Caleta Policarpo. Together, these pieces explore how objects, sounds, rituals, tools, vessels, ruins, and written accounts carry histories shaped by violence, labor, displacement, commerce, memory, and survival, while showing how seemingly isolated traces can open onto wider networks of cultural meaning and historical consequence.

(+) Abstract

This compilation brings together five texts that examine language politics, African history, pre-Columbian archaeology, transoceanic exchange, and artisanal labor through documents, objects, names, ships, and endangered practices. It opens with a reflection on Occitan and on the arguments often used to defend minoritized languages, criticizing the need to justify a language through antiquity, literary prestige, number of speakers, lexical richness, or comparison with dominant languages, and insisting instead that every language matters because it is the expressive medium of a human community. It then revisits the history of the Merina kingdom of Madagascar and the figure of Andrianampoinimerina, correcting older European historical accounts through more precise Malagasy-centered information about his political rise, reforms, territorial ambitions, administrative innovations, public works, legal codes, military expansion, and later symbolic destruction under French colonialism. Another text introduces the Coclé cultural area of present-day Panama, outlining its agricultural base, trade connections, goldwork, ceramics, funerary sites, social organization, and enduring archaeological mystery after the disruption caused by Spanish conquest. The compilation also reconstructs the story of the Date Maru, later known as the San Juan Bautista, one of the first Western-style galleons built in Japan, following its role in early seventeenth-century diplomatic, commercial, religious, and technological exchanges between Japan, New Spain, Rome, and the Philippines before Japan's closure to the outside world. The final text describes the traditional Galician tanning process in the curtidoiros, detailing the long sequence of soaking, liming, scraping, deliming, tanning with bark, greasing, drying, softening, and finishing hides, while contrasting artisanal knowledge with later industrial methods. Together, these pieces explore how cultural memory survives in threatened languages, rewritten histories, archaeological remains, maritime routes, technical vocabularies, and manual procedures, while emphasizing the need to read inherited sources critically and to recognize the knowledge embedded in practices often dismissed as local, obsolete, marginal, or merely practical.

This compilation brings together five texts that examine visual imagination, religious material culture, minoritized languages, political memory, and gender ideology through artworks, devotional objects, music, language persecution, and archival documents. It opens with the late Gothic altarpiece of Saint Michael by Joan de Rua, focusing on its strange Catalan demons as visual constructions of disorder, monstrosity, and the non-human within Iberian religious art. It then turns to Catalan alms boxes, especially those linked to souls in Purgatory, reading their painted wooden panels as small but powerful objects where doctrine, fear, charity, image, and ecclesiastical economy converged. Another text reflects on Asturian folk music through Na llende, connecting language, territory, childhood memory, political defeat, and the songs of anti-Franco fugitives who lived on the border between survival, resistance, and disappearance. The compilation also addresses the historical repression of Basque, tracing centuries of mockery, prohibition, punishment, institutional hostility, and political stigmatization, while defending the language as an integral part of Spain's cultural plurality rather than as a marker of suspicion or separatism. The final text uses misogynistic manuals and educational materials from Francoist Spain to expose the ideological construction of the "perfect woman" as obedient wife, domestic worker, sexual servant, mother, and subordinate intellectual presence, arguing implicitly for the role of libraries, archives, and museums in recovering such documents in order to critique the social structures they helped sustain. Together, these pieces explore how images, objects, songs, languages, and instructional texts preserve the traces of power: the power to frighten, persuade, discipline, erase, ridicule, remember, or resist.

(+) Abstract

This volume gathers a series of narrative essays that traverse languages, musical instruments, ritual practices, architectural landscapes, colonial encounters, botanical knowledge, and literary memory, weaving them into a reflective meditation on culture as lived archive. Moving from the semantic density of untranslatable words to Indigenous smoking mixtures in North America, from the Panamanian cucuá dancers to the mud "skyscrapers" of Shibam in Yemen, from Afro-Caribbean guloyas in the Dominican Republic to the figure of the Carib Queen in Trinidad, and from Amazonian exploration myths to Mark Twain's gastronomic recollections, the text explores how memory, identity, and material practice are embedded in objects, gestures, sounds, and built environments. Each chapter situates its subject historically and ethnographically while foregrounding the fragility of intangible heritage under conditions of colonial violence, modernization, environmental degradation, and global homogenization. Blending cultural history, travel narrative, archival reference, and literary commentary, the work treats shelves — literal and metaphorical — as sites where stories accumulate, overlap, and persist. It ultimately proposes reading as an act of cultural listening, and storytelling as a means of safeguarding plural pasts against erasure.

(+) Abstract

This essay presents four narrative vignettes that explore the role of elders as living repositories of memory, tradition, and cultural continuity in Latin American contexts. Through cases drawn from northeastern Argentina and Patagonia, it examines how musical practice, oral transmission, and endangered languages are sustained, transformed, or lost across generations. The figure of the chamamé musician Eustaquio Miño embodies the tension between innovation and custodianship, while Félix Medina's work among Qom communities foregrounds elders as irreplaceable "books" whose knowledge cannot be reduced to written form. The near disappearance of the Aónikenk language, preserved in its final fluent speakers, and the recorded chants of the Selk'nam shaman Lola Kiepja illustrate the fragility of intangible heritage in the face of colonial violence, displacement, and state-sanctioned erasure. By juxtaposing oral memory with institutional preservation efforts, the text interrogates hierarchies that privilege written archives over embodied knowledge and highlights the ethical urgency of listening, documentation, and intergenerational transmission. It ultimately argues that the survival of cultural identity depends not only on archival infrastructures but on sustained recognition of elders as epistemic authorities and active bearers of collective memory.

(+) Abstract

This sixteenth compilation assembles seven essays — "Ocean Island: cómo destruir una isla," "Los rostros en el barro," "Castelao y las cosas de la vida," "Diluvio en los Andes," "Sexo en Las Mil y una Noches," "Barreras," and "Más que mil palabras…" — that examine the entanglement of memory, material culture, and power across diverse historical and geographical contexts. The volume opens with a reconstruction of the environmental and social destruction of Banaba (Ocean Island) and Nauru under colonial phosphate extraction, exposing the ecological costs of industrial agriculture and imperial economic systems. It then turns to the ceramic traditions of the Diaguita peoples, analyzing anthropomorphic representations as carriers of identity and cultural continuity, and to the work of Alfonso Daniel Rodríguez Castelao, whose illustrated vignettes condense political critique, social observation, and vernacular knowledge within early twentieth-century Galician contexts. The compilation further engages with the Quechua manuscript of Huarochirí in "Diluvio en los Andes," foregrounding the complexities of translation, authorship, and linguistic hybridity in colonial textual production, as well as the persistence of Indigenous cosmologies within written archives shaped by missionary intervention. In "Sexo en Las Mil y una Noches," the focus shifts to Richard F. Burton’s translation of The Thousand and One Nights, examining the interplay between Orientalism, erotic discourse, censorship, and scholarly annotation within nineteenth-century European engagements with Middle Eastern narrative traditions. The final sections expand the analysis toward contemporary forms of representation and geopolitical structure. "Barreras" presents a catalog of physical border infrastructures — walls, fences, and demilitarized zones — mapping the materialization of political conflict and the persistence of division in a globalized world. "Más que mil palabras…" examines the satirical illustrations of Pawel Kuczynski as visual critiques of inequality, consumerism, and systemic violence, emphasizing the capacity of images to condense complex social commentary. Taken together, the compilation proposes an understanding of archives as dynamic and contested spaces, where landscapes, objects, texts, and images operate as carriers of memory shaped by extraction, translation, conflict, and creative expression.

"Los rostros de los Áña" documents Ava (Chiriguano) wooden masks carved from yuchán and used in the aréte abáti festival, reflecting on commodification, ecological pressure, and the tension between ritual object and market craft. "Las casas pintadas" describes the decorated adobe architecture of the Kassena in Tiébélé, Burkina Faso, foregrounding women's mural practices as both aesthetic expression and environmental protection. "Hebras de arco iris" traces the symbolic and practical uses of the Andean chumbe, from pre-Inca textiles to contemporary Misak, Nasa, and Inga traditions in southern Colombia. "Las tejedoras de crines" recounts the evolution of horsehair weaving in Rari, Chile, from indigenous fiberwork to UNESCO-recognized craft, highlighting adaptation under material scarcity and tourism. Across these entries, the compilation interrogates how objects — garments, instruments, masks, houses, belts, fibers — condense relations of power, cosmology, labor, and survival, and how their circulation transforms both meaning and community.

This nineteenth compilation includes the entries "Lea usted…" and "The Office Orchestra." The first examines early twentieth-century graphic advertising through a selection of illustrated announcements drawn from 1923 issues of the Spanish magazine La esfera. It analyzes advertisements for photographic devices (Foto-revólver Krauss, Goerz cameras, Kodak instant cameras), typewriters (Underwood, Corona), automobiles (Packard), pharmaceutical products (Elixir estomacal de Sáiz de Carlos, Delgadose Pesqui), hygiene brands (Colgate, Heno de Pravia), and popular fiction promoted under the imperative "Lea usted." "The Office Orchestra" documents a 1999 collaborative artwork by Andrea Chappell and Cherry Goddard, consisting of a cylindrical cardboard container housing an accordion-fold book that reassigns musical functions to ordinary office supplies — clips as "castaclips," elastic bands as "elastilutes," correction fluid containers as "correctoracas." The project integrates packaging design, book art, and participatory sound intervention, inviting the transformation of bureaucratic materials into instruments of collective play. Together, the entries reflect on print culture, design, and everyday objects as sites where commerce, creativity, and critique intersect.

Civallero, Edgardo (2022). Detenerse para innovar. Bibliotecario. [Link]

(+) Abstract

This essay critically reexamines the contemporary fixation on innovation within librarianship, arguing that the term has been diluted by technological novelty and spectacle. It proposes a redefinition of innovation as a situated, reflective, and problem-oriented process grounded in the concrete needs of communities rather than in the adoption of fashionable tools. Drawing on examples from Latin American libraries, the text contends that genuine innovation often emerges from scarcity rather than abundance, through creative reuse, adaptive strategies, and what might be described as pragmatic "tinkering" with available resources. Far from equating innovation with disruption or technological acceleration, the essay frames it as a deliberate act of critical pause: stopping to reassess institutional purposes, examine inherited practices, and realign action with core social commitments. Innovation, in this view, requires questioning underlying values, costs, exclusions, and long-term consequences, particularly in contexts marked by inequality and limited funding. By emphasizing reflection, community-rooted action, and the responsible reconfiguration of existing tools and practices, the work advances a model of innovation that privileges resilience, solidarity, and contextual intelligence over spectacle and market-driven technological determinism.

Civallero, Edgardo (2022). Su elección. Bibliotecario. [Link]

(+) Abstract

This essay examines the ideological construction of "choice" within contemporary neoliberal capitalism through a critical reading of global fast fashion and its labor regimes. Drawing on the documentary The True Cost and related testimonies, it analyzes how multinational corporations externalize production to impoverished regions, where deregulated labor conditions, environmental contamination, and structural dependency sustain the production of inexpensive consumer goods for Western markets. The text interrogates the rhetorical inversion through which exploitative labor arrangements are reframed as voluntary employment opportunities, emphasizing how discourses of development, market competition, and individual agency obscure coercive material realities. By juxtaposing corporate justifications with workers' testimonies and with theoretical reflections such as Catharine A. MacKinnon's critique of constrained choice, the essay argues that the language of "free decision" collapses under conditions where structural inequality eliminates viable alternatives. It situates fast fashion within broader patterns of neocolonial extraction and moral outsourcing, demonstrating how consumer societies normalize distant suffering through narratives of inevitability and progress. The work ultimately challenges the ethical neutrality of consumption and calls into question the legitimacy of invoking individual choice in contexts defined by systemic deprivation and asymmetrical power.

Civallero, Edgardo (2021). Pensamiento bibliotecológico crítico. Bibliotecario. [Link]

(+) Abstract

This essay advances a critical rethinking of librarianship from a Latin American perspective, interrogating the persistent colonial, classist, and epistemic hierarchies embedded in library institutions and professional discourse. Beginning with a critique of the widespread notion of libraries as neutral "places of knowledge," it argues that historically dominant models in the region have privileged written, academic, and Eurocentric forms of knowledge while marginalizing oral traditions, Indigenous and Afro-descendant memories, non-textual supports, and community-based epistemologies. Through reflective analysis grounded in lived professional experience, the text exposes the structural exclusions reproduced by library policies, architectural models, and collection practices, and questions the celebratory rhetoric surrounding monumental "libraries of the future" that align with neoliberal spectacle rather than social need. It further proposes that critical librarianship must acknowledge the role of emotion — anger, grief, frustration, hope — as legitimate epistemic forces in professional reflection and action, particularly within contexts marked by inequality and systemic neglect. Extending its critique beyond information institutions to broader extractivist and industrial logics, the essay situates librarianship within global structures of capitalist production and environmental degradation, arguing for territorially rooted, socially committed, and ecologically responsible practices. The work ultimately calls for a plural, decolonial, and community-centered reconfiguration of libraries as spaces capable of sustaining diverse knowledges and confronting the political conditions that shape access to information.
