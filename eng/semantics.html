Semantics

Applied semantics is far more than a technical concern. It is a way of structuring what we know —and what we have forgotten— through systems capable of linking, preserving, and reusing knowledge across formats, disciplines, and time. This section presents a small and deliberately unusual part of my work as a semantic consultant specializing in ontology development, metadata modeling, and the design of data infrastructures for institutions managing scientific collections, archival records, and biocultural memory.

I design ontologies in Protégé, build RDF pipelines in JSON-LD, reconcile datasets with OpenRefine and RDFLib, and apply standards like Darwin Core, MODS, ABCD, EML, and Dublin Core. I also work with FAIR and CARE principles, and explore instruments like TK Labels to preserve provenance, multilingual meaning, and data sovereignty.

What appears here, however, belongs to a different register: exploratory projects and speculative models — critical detours that examine what formal systems tend to exclude. I use the same tools but ask different questions: How do ontologies encode silence? What happens when metadata grieves? Can we model what never made it into the archive?

As I explain in the Key Topics section, semantics is neither neutral nor innocent. Classifying, naming, and modeling the world are political acts. My approach moves between institutional transformation and epistemic composting — allowing dead structures to decompose and nourish other logics.
