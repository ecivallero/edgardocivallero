The Bi-Weekly Summary | 03-16.Nov.2025

Federated Meanings, Symbiotic Archives, and the Violence of the Written Interface

What binds a micro-thesaurus, a fungal network, an inter-institutional metadata exchange, and a person who cannot read the signs in a public library? During the past two weeks, I traced a single, continuous line across these disparate terrains: the politics of relation. Whether the setting is semantic modeling, ecological analogy, or the ethics of access, each piece interrogates how systems remember, connect, and exclude — and how meaning emerges not from isolated entities but from the infrastructures that allow them to coexist.

In Micro-Thesauri and Relational Logics, the Metadata as Revolt series moves from sabotage to construction — from exposing the violence of universality to outlining a practical architecture of plurality. Here, the macro-vocabularies that dominate global knowledge infrastructures (LCSH, AAT, Darwin Core) are revealed not as neutral harmonizers but as machines of semantic compression. Against them, the text proposes micro-thesauri: small, situated, accountable vocabularies whose authority derives from context rather than scale. Their governing principle is not inheritance but relation — a shift that transforms metadata from hierarchy to negotiation. What emerges is a federated semantic ecology built from proximity, provenance, and explicit mapping. A quiet revolution of scale.

From semantic federations the reflection turns toward ecological ones. Interoperability as Symbiosis reframes a foundational concept of digital librarianship by breaking it open along ethical and biological lines. Interoperability, long reduced to a technical achievement, becomes here an ecological condition: a form of co-existence in which institutions, records, and users survive through interdependence. Mechanical alignment is replaced by interpretive translation; provenance becomes polyprovenancial; preservation becomes the art of sustaining relation through change. The proposal is radical: treat interoperability as a mode of care, a mycelial ethics of linked memory in which stability arises not from uniformity but from adaptive entanglement.

That same entanglement is explored narratively in the chronicle Nothing Stands Alone, where the Andean cloud forest becomes a mirror for the archive’s future. Mycorrhizal networks — the fungal infrastructure through which forests share nutrients and warnings — offer a biological grammar for post-custodial memory systems. Meaning, that text suggests, is not an intrinsic property but a metabolic relation; documents live only insofar as they circulate. The chronicle extends the theoretical work by grounding it in ecological presence: decay as continuity, redundancy as resilience, and connection as the fundamental unit of survival. This is not metaphor as ornament; it is metaphor as method, a reorientation of archival thought through the epistemic logic of the forest floor.

Finally, The Illiterate User confronts a different kind of relation: the one libraries too often refuse. If the previous texts imagine infrastructures that thrive on plurality, this note exposes the institutional spaces that impose a singular way of being: literate, textual, compliant. The library, cloaked in hospitality, assumes reading as the price of admission. Illiteracy becomes defect; the user becomes problem; shame becomes policy. Against this, the piece calls for a library that does not require textual obedience — a space where knowledge can be exchanged through voice, gesture, memory, or community; where access is not contingent on literacy; where the right not to read is recognized as legitimate. This critique is not ancillary to the previous themes: it is their ethical anchor. If micro-thesauri distribute authority and mycelial archives distribute survival, then a non-literate library must distribute dignity.

Across these four works, a single proposition crystallizes: that memory, meaning, and access are never properties of isolated objects or individuals, but always the outcome of relations — semantic, ecological, social. The task ahead is to design infrastructures that honor that truth: systems that do not require homogenization to connect, or literacy to include; systems that metabolize change rather than deny it; systems that speak in many registers, and listen in many more.



Navsuv offers a biweekly synthesis of my work: blog posts, critical notes, articles, archival documents, and other materials. It's not just a summary — it's an editorial thread tracing how each piece fits into a shifting landscape of memory, critique, and resistance.

The name comes from the language of the Sivdara, a fictional people from a broader personal project I'm developing: a continent imagined as a space to explore knowledge, memory, and the tension between presence and erasure. In their tongue, navsuv names the rope-and-straw suspension bridges used to cross the naroow valleys, up in the mountains — ephemeral yet enduring paths stretched between worlds. Like this section.
